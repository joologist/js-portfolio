City of SD - Get It Done

Last December, I was selected as one of 11 students to work on a UX audit project with Fi @ UCSD for the city of San Diego.
As a UX consultant, I worked on user research, usability testing, and designing prototypes!

ROLE UX Researcher, UX Designer

TEAM Fi @ UCSD

TIMEFRAME 14 weeks (January - May 2020)

TOOLS Figma, Asana

Background

The city of San Diego runs a service called Get It Done, a platform where residents can report city issues like potholes or broken street lights.

Here's a quick rundown of GID: reports can be submitted through mobile and web, but most users use their phones to make quick reports. If they provide their contact information, the city will send them updates on the issue and hopefully resolve it! Fi partnered with SD's Performance & Analytics Department to see where this process could be improved.



Phase 1:
User Research

We split into 3 subteams to focus on the 3 phases of Get It Done: Pre-Reporting, Reporting, & Tracking. My subteam researched the Reporting phase, and you can find our report here. TLDR: we conducted surveys, ran usability tests, and came up with these takeaways:

1. The app currently has no verification stage.

While the website has four stages to making a report, the app only has one. The lack of a verification stage can cause mistakes, and the current platform doesn't support edits, updates, or deletion.







2. The language used for the same issues on mobile and web is inconsistent.

Picking the correct issue can be confusing for a user and disrupts the efficiency of the reporting process.

3. Users aren’t sure what the city will do with their contact information and are less inclined to share it.

This small message on the reporting page isn't noticeable, and without contact info, the city has no way to follow up on these issues.



Phase 2:
Solution Research

In our next phase, I was on the Expert Review subteam while the rest of the team worked on creating personas, storyboards, and competitive analysis.

My two teammates and I compiled 16 pages of usability issues, primarily identifying misleading labels, lack of error prevention, and lack of user guidance. Based off our 5 weeks of research, we had 3 design goals moving forward:

1. Decrease report duplication & ensure report validity.

2. Allow the community to provide report updates.

3. Increase communication about report progress.

Phase 3:
Designing It!

To prepare for our redesign, our team made paper prototypes, conducted more usability testing, and went through several iterations. In this final phase, I led the User Guidance subteam and was the main designer for "All Reports"!
